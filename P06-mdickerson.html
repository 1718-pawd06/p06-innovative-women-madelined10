Biography

"Elizabeth Blackwell was the first woman to graduate from medical school in the United States. She graduated top of her class at Geneva Medical College. She also became a leading public health activist during her lifetime. Blackwell was inspired to pursue medicine by a dying friend who said her ordeal would have been better had she had a female physician. Most male physicians trained as apprentices to experienced doctors; there were few medical colleges and none that accepted women, though a few women. In the mid-1850s, she opened a clinic that became known as the New York Dispensary for Poor Women and Children. With help from her sister and fellow doctor Emily Blackwell, who worked as a surgeon. Elizabeth Blackwell also established the New York Infirmary for Indigent Women and Children in 1857, an institution that would last for more than a century. She also created a medical school for women in the late 1860s. Blackwell returned to Europe and worked in London and Paris. She focused on midwifery at La Maternité, where she contracted a disease during a procedure on an infant that left her blind in one eye; she was thus unable to practice surgery as she had wished. Blackwell later returned to New York City and established a private practice, at first struggling financially again due to the prejudices of the day. Dr. Blackwell traveled widely across Europe and became increasingly interested in social reform movements dedicated to women’s rights, family planning, hygiene, eugenics, medical education, sexual purity and Christian socialism."

Accomplishments

1847

Geneva College in New York agreed to accept Elizabeth as a student.


1849

Blackwell graduated from Medical School, and moved to Paris to continue to practice medicine.


1857

She opened her own practice for women and children in New York.


1861

The Civil War begins, and Blackwell trains many women to be nurses for the wounded soldiers.


1868

She opened her own practice for women in both America and England.


1871

Blackwell becomes a published author with her book, The Religion of Health.


1875

She became a professor of gynecology at the London School of Medicine.
